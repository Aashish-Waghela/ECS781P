Threat Notification

New Android malware 'WolfRAT' targets social media applications and possibly used for cyberespionage

Malware

Type: Incident | Issue Date : 21-05-2020 | TLP: Green | Industry: All | Region: Asia-Pacific | Serial: G-TN-EN-01-12993

Summary

On May 19, 2020 security vendor Cisco Talos published a report detailing an attack campaign targeting Android users in Taiwan with a Remote Access Trojan (RAT) dubbed "WolfRAT." The Android malware tries to target popular social media applications such as WhatsApp, Facebook, and Line Messenger to perform cyberespionage. WolfRAT is based on leaked source code of the Dendroid Android malware, which researchers initially discovered in 2014. Security researchers believe the Trojan is still under active development and observed at least four major releases. It was suspected that the initial infection vector was phishing links or fake update lures. Upon successful installation, the Trojan spoofs a legitimate service. In at least one of the instances, it was observed that WolfRAT leveraged a package titled "com.google.services" to give the false impression as being related to the legitimate Google service.

 

According to Cisco Talos researchers, WolfRAT is simpler compared to other Android malware as it does not take advantage of the accessibility framework for implementing its functionalities and can collect basic information from non-rooted devices (i.e. it doesn't need root access with highest privileges on the compromised device). Some of the functionalities such as capture photo, capture video, record audio, retrieve sent SMS messages, and upload pictures, are focused on espionage activities.

 

Researchers have attributed WolfRAT to the Wolf Research group, a Germany based organization selling various spyware tools. This group was previously profiled by security researchers as developers of interception and espionage-based malware. Two security researchers publicly exposed the Wolf Research group during a presentation at the 2018 Virus Bulletin conference. As a result, the organization faced a shutdown; however, it was reported that the perpetrators surfaced back with a new organization branded as LokD (currently headquartered in Cyprus). WolfRAT developers show amateur development methodology as they constantly add or remove various packages, utilize deprecated techniques, and duplicate its content from various open-source projects.

Recommendations

Deloitte CTI recommends to do the following:

·        Monitor network for given IOCs and block them.

·        For employees, alert the IT security team of potentially suspicious emails and files.

·        Download android applications from trusted sources only.

·        Be mindful of the permissions being provided to the application while installing any new application on your device.
